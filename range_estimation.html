Range Estimation/Scope Usage

Overview

This section will give you some general knowledge and understanding on how to use mildots for any scope. This also goes over how to quickly find a fairly accurate estimation of range if you ever find yourself without those trusty rangefinders.

What is a mildot?

First things first, a “mildot” is an abbreviation for milliradian also known as an MRAD or mil for short. A milliradian accounts for 1/1000 of a radian.

What is a radian?

A radian is a unit of angle that is located at the center of a circle and its arc is equal in length to the radius of the circle. Essentially, this is the distance that is traveled around the circle.

Mathematical Basics

It has been argued about how many milliradians are in a complete circle. The Russian army says there are 6,000 milliradians in a circle while the US Army says a circle has 6,400 milliradians for some unknown reason.

Pi is half of a circle which is equal to 3.14159265359 radians so multiply that by 2 and you get 6.28318530718 radians, so in milliradians that would be approximately, 6,283 milliradians in a circle. We’re just going to round for easier math.

Rapid Range Estimation Trick

What you need to do first is look down your scope, zoom in as much as your scope will let you if at all possible, then you need to estimate the height of your target in meters, multiply that number by 1000. We will call this number X. Now what you need to do is use any one of the scope techniques I talked about earlier to find the height of your target in mildots. Once you have that number, divide your number X by the mildot height of your target. This will give a rough estimation of the distance to your target.

Example:

  1. Target is 1.8 meters tall (average height of an adult male)
  2. 1.8 x 1000 = 1800
  3. Target is 3 mildots high
  4. 1800/3 = 600 yards to target

Note: On a traditional scope the reticle will look like a series of mildots going perfectly vertically and horizontally through the scope. The distance between these dots is equal to exactly 1 mil. On the right are a few more examples on how to find range with different units of measurement.

Quick Tip For Using Mildots

So when you are looking through the scope, you can use the mildots for quick adjustment to a target. Say you have to adjust up 2 mildots for a target at a distance. Instead of making a certain amount of clicks to your scope to accommodate for the distance, you can simply put the second mildot below the crosshairs on the target and you should be able to hit the target. This isn’t as accurate as using clicks on the scope but is still a fast and effective technique to use in a pinch.

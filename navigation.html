Map Reading & Navigation

Overview

It is very important to be able to read a map effectively so you can either locate yourself on the battlefield or mark and find a location of interest on the map. Having adept knowledge on how to use a map to your advantage is a must and can also prevent you from wandering aimlessly throughout the land if you are ever seperated from your unit.

Contour Lines:

These are the several lines on a map that represent the elevation of an area on a map.

Contour Interval:

The contour interval refers to the vertical elevation between two or more contour lines and is used to determine the slope of an area on the map.

Five Major Terrain Features

(Hidden Valley Ranch Salad Dressing)

  1. Hill
  2. Valley
  3. Ridge
  4. Saddle
  5. Depression

Hill – A raised area of land, has some elevation but not as much as a mountain.

Valley – level ground that has higher ground on at least two sides. May or may not contain a stream/river.

Ridge – high ground that has varying height differences along a crest. The crest is higher than the ground on all sides of the ridge.

Saddle – A low point that is located in between two high points along the crest of a ridge.

Depression – A low point on a map that has higher ground surrounding it on all sides.

Three Minor Terrain Features

  1. Draw
  2. Spur
  3. Cliff

Draw - ground that is surrounded by higher ground on each side and the ground slopes upward towards the head of the draw.

Spur - Usually formed by two streams creating draws on both sides. A sloping line of higher ground that is usually extending out on a ridgeline.

Cliff - A severe vertical drop in elevation usually marked by very tight contour lines. Tick points towards lower ground

What is an Azimuth?

Without getting very technical with crazy terms that makes things hard to understand, think of an azimuth as an imaginary, perfectly linear line that starts at your compass and goes in the direction you are pointing. There is a number on your compass that is associated with this imaginary line and that is your azimuth.

Converting Azimuths

It is important in land navigation to know how to convert azimuths so you can effectively navigate your way through any terrain. If you need to convert any azimuth you’ll need to know something that is called the G-M angle and this is found on every map in the legend and varies based on where you are in the world. For converting from a grid azimuth to magnetic azimuth, add the value of the G-M angle to the grid azimuth. If you want to convert the other way, magnetic azimuth to grid azimuth, you simply subtract the G-M angle from the magnetic azimuth.

Intersection

Intersection – This method is used when you want to know the location of an unknown point on the map by using two other known points.

  1. First when using a map, you want to orient the map with the compass and then mark your position on the map. (If not known use resection to find your location)
  2. Take the magnetic azimuth of the unknown position using your compass
  3. Your compass gives you what is called a magnetic azimuth and the azimuth on the map is called a grid azimuth. You’ll need to convert from the magnetic azimuth to the grid azimuth.
  4. You will now draw a line on the map along the grid azimuth that goes through your position.
  5. You will now move to the second known location on the map and repeat the steps up to this step.
  6. The location of the unknown point is where these two lines intersect on the map. (Use more than two known locations for more accurate result but you’ll need at least two known points)

Resection

  1. Note: You will want to use the resection method when you do not know your current location on a map and need to locate your position.
  2. Similar to the Intersection method, you’ll want to orient the map using your compass and then identify at least two locations that you can visually see and mark them on your map.
  3. Convert the magnetic azimuth to the grid azimuth, then get a back azimuth using your grid azimuth.
  4. Draw a line on the map with the back azimuth you obtained from the known location back toward your unknown location.
  5. Repeat this process for the second known position.
  6. Where these two lines intersect is your position on the map.
